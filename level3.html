Level 3

Research Without Blind Trust

AI can sound confident and still be wrong. This level teaches you how to use AI responses safely.
What you will have by the end:One verification habit or checklist you will use every time
The lesson

The confidence problem

AI tools do not know when they are wrong. They generate responses that sound fluent and authoritative whether the information is accurate or not. This is sometimes called hallucination — the AI produces something plausible but incorrect.

This does not mean AI is useless. It means you need a habit for checking what it gives you before you act on it — especially for anything that involves facts, numbers, names, dates, or claims you will share with others.

The good news: checking AI responses does not have to be slow. A simple three-step habit covers most situations.

What to watch for

Three things AI gets wrong most often

Knowing where AI tends to go wrong helps you know what to check.

Specific facts and numbers
Statistics, percentages, dates, prices, and named studies are high-risk. AI generates plausible-sounding figures that may be invented.
Names and quotes
AI sometimes attributes quotes or credentials to real people incorrectly. Always verify before sharing.
Recent events
AI training data has a cutoff date. Anything recent — news, updated policies, new research — may be missing or wrong.
The checking habit

Your three-step checking habit

You do not need to verify everything word for word. You need a consistent habit that catches the things that matter.

  1. 1
    Read it like a human, not a machine
    Does it actually make sense? Does it answer what you asked? Would you be comfortable putting your name on it?
  2. 2
    Check any specific facts independently
    If the response contains statistics, names, dates, or claims — look them up. Use a search engine or a source you trust.
  3. 3
    Apply your own knowledge
    You know your work better than AI does. Does this response fit your situation? Does anything feel off?
What this means practically

When to check and when it is fine to trust

Check carefully: Anything with specific facts, statistics, names, dates, legal or medical information, or anything you will publish or share with others.

Lower risk: Drafting a structure or outline, brainstorming options, summarising something you already understand, or writing a first draft you will review yourself.

The task you identified in Level 1 will tell you which category it falls into. Most beginner AI use cases — drafting emails, summarising documents, creating templates — are lower risk. Still worth a quick read-through, but not a deep fact-check.

Your Level 3 activity

Build your checking habit

You are going to create your own simple verification checklist — three to five questions you will ask yourself every time you use an AI response for real work. Then submit it using the activity form.

  1. 1
    Think about your Level 1 task
    What could go wrong if the AI gave you incorrect information for that task? What would you need to check before using the response?
  2. 2
    Write your checklist
    Write three to five questions you will ask yourself before using any AI response. Example: Does this contain any specific facts or numbers I should verify? Does this sound like something I would actually say or write?
  3. 3
    Keep it somewhere useful
    Save your checklist somewhere you will actually see it — a sticky note, a phone note, a document. You will use it in every level from here.
  4. 4
    Submit your checklist
    Paste your checklist into the activity form below.
